# 🎬 Film History — Major Movements & Eras

## Silent Era (1895–1927)
- **Key figures**: Georges Méliès, D.W. Griffith, Buster Keaton, Charlie Chaplin, F.W. Murnau
- **Defining films**: *The Birth of a Nation* (1915), *Nosferatu* (1922), *The General* (1926), *Sunrise* (1927)
- **Innovation**: Narrative editing, continuity, intertitles, expressionist lighting
- **Peak**: German Expressionism (Caligari, Nosferatu, Metropolis)

## Classical Hollywood (1927–1960)
- **Studio system**: MGM, Paramount, Warner Bros., 20th Century Fox, RKO
- **Golden Age**: 1939 — *Gone with the Wind*, *The Wizard of Oz*, *Stagecoach*
- **Genre refinement**: Western, noir, musical, screwball comedy
- **Key directors**: John Ford, Howard Hawks, Alfred Hitchcock, Billy Wilder
- **Technical**: Synchronized sound (1927), Technicolor (1939), CinemaScope (1953)

## French New Wave (1959–1967)
- **Cahiers du Cinéma critics turned directors**: Godard, Truffaut, Rohmer, Chabrol, Rivette
- **Style**: Jump cuts, handheld camera, documentary realism, direct address
- **Key films**: *Breathless* (1960), *The 400 Blows* (1959), *Jules and Jim* (1962)
- **Influence**: Liberated global cinema from studio conventions

## Italian Neorealism (1943–1952)
- **Post-war authenticity**: Non-professional actors, location shooting, working-class stories
- **Key directors**: Roberto Rossellini, Vittorio De Sica, Luchino Visconti
- **Key films**: *Rome, Open City* (1945), *Bicycle Thieves* (1948), *Umberto D.* (1952)
- **Influence**: Indirectly shaped Indian Parallel Cinema, Iranian New Wave

## Japanese Golden Age (1950s–1960s)
- **Key directors**: Akira Kurosawa, Yasujirō Ozu, Kenji Mizoguchi, Masaki Kobayashi
- **Key films**: *Seven Samurai* (1954), *Tokyo Story* (1953), *Ugetsu* (1953), *Harakiri* (1962)
- **Studio system**: Toho, Daiei, Shochiku, Nikkatsu
- **Influence**: Kurosawa inspired *Star Wars*, *The Magnificent Seven*, *A Fistful of Dollars*

## New Hollywood (1967–1980)
- **Auteur era**: Directors gained creative control as studios collapsed
- **Key directors**: Francis Ford Coppola, Martin Scorsese, Robert Altman, William Friedkin, Hal Ashby
- **Key films**: *The Godfather* (1972), *Taxi Driver* (1976), *Nashville* (1975), *The French Connection* (1971)
- **End**: *Heaven's Gate* (1980) — the film that killed the auteur renaissance
- **Transition**: Blockbuster era begins with *Jaws* (1975) and *Star Wars* (1977)

## New German Cinema (1966–1982)
- **Key directors**: Rainer Werner Fassbinder, Werner Herzog, Wim Wenders, Margarethe von Trotta
- **Key films**: *Aguirre, the Wrath of God* (1972), *The Marriage of Maria Braun* (1979), *Paris, Texas* (1984)
- **Themes**: Post-war German identity, alienation, American influence

## Hong Kong New Wave (1979–1997)
- **Key directors**: Wong Kar-wai, John Woo, Tsui Hark, Ringo Lam
- **Key films**: *Chungking Express* (1994), *Hard Boiled* (1992), *A Better Tomorrow* (1986)
- **Influence**: Woo's gun-fu redefined action cinema globally

## Iranian New Wave (1969–present)
- **Key directors**: Abbas Kiarostami, Mohsen Makhmalbaf, Asghar Farhadi, Jafar Panahi
- **Key films**: *Close-Up* (1990), *A Separation* (2011), *Taste of Cherry* (1997)
- **Style**: Poetic realism, documentary blur, children as protagonists, minimalism

## Korean New Wave (1996–present)
- **Key directors**: Bong Joon-ho, Park Chan-wook, Lee Chang-dong, Kim Ki-duk
- **Key films**: *Oldboy* (2003), *Parasite* (2019), *Burning* (2018), *Memories of Murder* (2003)
- **Themes**: Class struggle, revenge, trauma, identity
- **Industry**: Korean film subsidies post-1996, Busan International Film Festival

## Digital Age (2000–present)
- **Key shifts**: Digital cameras → democratization of filmmaking
- **Streaming era**: Netflix, Amazon, Apple disrupt distribution
- **Global cinema**: International co-productions, Oscar international feature growth
- **Important contemporary directors**: PTA, Wes Anderson, Denis Villeneuve, Greta Gerwig, Jordan Peele, Bong Joon-ho
